Screenplay: How to Write

by Blasius N. Marvin

For scene headings

INT. [or] EXT. [or] INT./EXT. [where it takes place] - [time]

Examples :

INT. NAME'S HOUSE - DAY
    EXT. NAME'S HOUSE - DAY
    INT./EXT. NAME'S CAR - DAY
    INT. PLACE - A YEAR LATER (timeskip to said time)
    INT. PLACE - MOMENTS LATER (same stuff)

Screenplay scene headings (INT. and EXT.) tell us where the camera is, not the characters. This means EXT. indicates an outdoor shot, even if we're looking at something inside through a window.

For scenes that transition between inside and outside, use INT./EXT. This applies to cars too, even if the scene focuses on the interior.

Finally, hallways are considered interior locations, even though they're inside a building. You can use INT. CLARA'S APARTMENT BUILDING - HALLWAY - DAY or INT. OUTSIDE CLARA'S APARTMENT - HALLWAY - DAY.

learn more at https://scriptwrecked.com/2020/02/25/int-and-ext-sure-youre-using-them-correctly/.

Scene headings, pt. ii. Time...

...is really simple to mention. There are four types of time (all written IN CAPS):

  • - DAY,

  • - NIGHT,

  • - DUSK, and

  • - DAWN.

...and some time passage, maybe?

  • - SEVERAL SECONDS LATER,

  • - SEVERAL MOMENTS LATER,

  • - SEVERAL HOURS LATER,

  • - SEVERAL DAYS LATER,

  • - (number of) DAYS LATER,

  • - SEVERAL MONTHS LATER,

  • - (number of) MONTHS LATER,

  • - SEVERAL YEARS LATER,

  • - (number of) YEARS LATER,

  • ∞ ...and more! ∞

Time is flexible here, but they are the commoners.

For dialogue,

you have to write the name in ALL CAPS Example: 

NAME (actions while they say in this line)
    Things that they're saying

    Right, it's always about your mom... you always take her
